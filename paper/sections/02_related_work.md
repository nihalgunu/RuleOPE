# 2  Related work

## 2.1 Off-policy evaluation in contextual bandits

The classical OPE triptych — Direct Method (DM), Inverse Propensity
Scoring (IPS; Horvitz and Thompson 1952; Rosenbaum and Rubin 1983), and
the Doubly Robust estimator (DR; Robins et al.\ 1994; Dudík, Langford,
and Li 2011) — forms the starting point of every modern OPE paper.
DM is biased when the reward regression is misspecified. IPS is
unbiased under positivity but has variance that scales with the
inverse of the minimum propensity. DR combines the two and is
consistent whenever *either* the regression or the propensity is
correctly specified.

Subsequent work has focused on variance reduction. Switch-DR (Wang et
al.\ 2017) reduces to DM when the importance weight explodes; More
Robust DR (Farajtabar et al.\ 2018) additionally corrects the
regression; Shrinkage DR (Su et al.\ 2020) tempers the weight.
Self-normalised IPS (SNIPS; Swaminathan and Joachims 2015) normalises
by the empirical weight mass.

All of the above assume a *stochastic* logging policy with known
propensities and positivity. None directly addresses the deterministic
logging regime in which production RAG pipelines operate.

## 2.2 Deterministic-logging OPE

Saito et al.\ (2022, 2023) study OPE when the action space is large or
continuous and propose kernel-smoothing estimators that reinterpret the
deterministic target policy as a kernel around the logged action. The
`CIPS` (clipped IPS) and `CIPS-DR` estimators in our comparison are
representative of this line of work. They provide a principled bias-
variance tradeoff but do not exploit combinatorial structure of the
action (rule) space.

Kallus and Uehara (2020) propose a "double reinforcement learning"
estimator for OPE with deterministic policies in sequential decision
problems. Our setting is stateless so we do not instantiate that
framework.

## 2.3 OPE for ranking and slates

Li et al.\ (2015) introduced position-dependent IPS for slate
recommendation; Swaminathan et al.\ (2017) and Vlassis et al.\ (2019)
generalise. Cascade DR (Kiyohara et al.\ 2022; McInerney et al.\ 2020)
factorises the reward over positions under a cascade user model. We
compare against Cascade DR because its factorisation over positions is
the closest existing analogue to our factorisation over atoms. The
difference: Cascade DR factorises over *positions in the output slate*,
whereas RuleOPE factorises over *features of the input context*. The
former assumes a cascade user who consumes positions sequentially; the
latter assumes that the reward function is approximately linear in
context indicators, which is the natural assumption in our feature
vocabulary.

## 2.4 RAG evaluation

Most of the RAG evaluation literature is concerned with *on-policy*
evaluation: automatic benchmarks such as KILT (Petroni et al.\ 2021),
BEIR (Thakur et al.\ 2021), HotpotQA (Yang et al.\ 2018); LLM-as-judge
grading; faithfulness metrics (ARES, Saad-Falcon et al.\ 2023; RAGAS,
Es et al.\ 2023). These answer "how well does this pipeline do on this
benchmark" but not "how will swapping component $X$ change the reward
distribution." Our work is complementary: RuleOPE consumes logs
generated by any RAG pipeline (including those benchmarked by the above)
and estimates the reward under candidate interventions.

Asai et al.\ (2023) and Jiang et al.\ (2023) propose adaptive retrieval
strategies that gate retrieval on query features; these are
architectural analogues of our "rules". The literature does not
currently offer an offline evaluation protocol for deciding whether a
specific gating rule should be shipped.

## 2.5 Counterfactual reasoning under sparse correction signals

Expert corrections as a source of partial counterfactual identification
appears in the medical-decision literature (Joachims et al.\ 2018;
Zhang et al.\ 2020) and in the learning-from-feedback literature
(Shivaswamy and Joachims 2015). Our correction-fusion term is
conceptually related but targets a different object: we impute the
counterfactual reward under an alternative action *only on records
where a correction was issued*, and use the imputation inside a DR
correction rather than in the primary regression. This formulation, to
our knowledge, is new.

## 2.6 Off-policy rule learning

The closest work in rule-learning-with-off-policy-evaluation is
probably Swaminathan and Joachims's (2015) Counterfactual Risk
Minimisation (CRM) framework, which treats policy learning as ERM on an
IPS-weighted risk. Our work is about *evaluation*, not learning: we
assume a candidate rule set (produced by any generator, including
automated rule mining or human engineers) and estimate each rule's
value. The learning analogue is a natural extension discussed in §10.
